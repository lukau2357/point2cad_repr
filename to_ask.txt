❯ The manual pathaway works, I've already established that. Are there any references to this algorithm in the literature, where it is explained in detail?                              

Yes, this is a well-known algorithm in computational geometry. The core idea — at each vertex, pick the next edge by angular ordering (first CCW from the incoming direction) to trace
  face boundaries — is the standard algorithm for computing faces of a planar subdivision. It's equivalent to traversing a DCEL (Doubly-Connected Edge List) or half-edge data
structure.

Key references:

1. de Berg, van Kreveld, Overmars, Schwarzkopf — "Computational Geometry: Algorithms and Applications" (Springer). Chapter 2 covers the DCEL and how face traversal works by following
  "twin" and "next" half-edges. The angular ordering at vertices is exactly how the DCEL's next-pointers are defined.
2. Mäntylä — "An Introduction to Solid Modeling" (Computer Science Press, 1988). Directly about BRep construction. Covers Euler operators and how face loops (wires) are assembled
from edges, including the distinction between outer and inner loops.
3. Hoffmann — "Geometric and Solid Modeling" (Morgan Kaufmann, 1989). Chapter on boundary representations, including wire assembly from edge graphs on surfaces.
4. Preparata & Shamos — "Computational Geometry: An Introduction". Covers planar subdivisions and face enumeration.

The most directly applicable is Mäntylä for the BRep context, and de Berg et al. for the algorithmic foundations. Your implementation is essentially: embed the edge graph on the
surface, compute angular ordering at each vertex using the surface tangent plane, then extract face cycles by always following the first CCW neighbor — which is the textbook DCEL
face traversal adapted to curved surfaces.
